The Paper Pigeon

Canadian Financial Autonomy - A Suffragette Ripple

One of the greatest strengths of the UK Suffragette movement was their ability to get noticed. Their use of direct action and embodiment of Deeds not words, made them a perfect headliner in the press. As we know, if it bleeds, it leads. This use of “negative” press to their advantage is in fact where they got their name from. “Suffragette” was a term coined by the Daily Mail intended to be derogatory, but the members of the WSPU embraced it full heartedly. Not only did this allow the group to gain members very quickly, but it also let them be an inspiration for other groups fighting for women’s rights, even all the way across the Atlantic in Canada.

Canadian Women’s Labour Leagues (WLLs) started in the early 1900s, during the time when the Suffragettes were most active. They fought primarily for women’s financial independence and autonomy, and many WLLs used Suffragette-esc tactics. Helen Armstrong was the leader of the Winnipeg Women’s Labour League, and she was a champion of direct action. During the 1919 Winnipeg General Strike, she was called the “business manager of the Women’s Unions” by the Toronto Star. In addition to participating in the strike, she was one of only two women on the strike committee, and actively harassed strike-breakers, for which she was jailed. This would be the second time she was arrested in as many months. While it is impossible to know if the Winnipeg Women’s Labour League was directly inspired by the Suffragettes, it is safe to say that both movement’s use of press and ability to “get noticed” through direct action significantly contributed to each group’s progress.

The Suffragettes primarily fought for women’s right to vote, and that is because a right to vote can lead to a right to many other things. Marie-Claire Kirkland was the first women to be part of the Legislative Assembly of Quebec. In 1964, she was able to pass An Act respecting the legal capacity of married women, often referred to simply as Bill 16, which, among other things, made is so that married women in Quebec could now open their own bank accounts without their husband’s permission. This is an odd case of non-violent direct action, but its lack of violence was only made possible because of the work of previous activists. Otherwise, Kirkland wouldn’t have been in Quebec’s assembly at all.

There are countless other examples of “ripples” created by the Suffragettes. Due to the group’s ability to consistently get on the front page of newspapers, their indirect effect throughout the 20th century has been massive. If we can learn one thing from this, perhaps it’s that in order to change the rules, you have to break them.
