The Paper Pigeon

Men's Birth Control

Photo Source: https://www.singlecare.com/blog/male-birth-control-options/

If you look at the science alone, male birth control should already exist, as a real, everyday option. The issue isn’t that nothing works. It’s that everything that does work comes with something that crosses it out as an option. A side effect or a condition that society deems inexcusable.

A lot of early attempts were happenstance. Drugs like thioridazine, used for schizophrenia, and phenoxybenzamine, used for blood pressure, started messing with fertility in ways no one planned. Both of them through different methods created an “invisible orgasm.” Technically, both could prevent pregnancy. However, they messed with major systems like the heart and blood pressure, so they weren’t safe to give to healthy people. These “accidents” led to new ideas for how to design a drug that actually can be used. However the idea of always having an invisible orgasm and physically being incapable of ejaculation threatened men’s ideas of masculinity enough to stop the trails.

A more direct attempt came with a drug called WIN-18,446 that was originally targeted towards Parasitic Worms. It worked by locking an enzyme called Aldehyde Dehydrogenase (ALDH). That enzyme helps convert vitamin A into retinoic acid. Without it, sperm production basically shuts down. The idea was simple and it worked well. In many cases, it was even reversible. The problem was that the same enzyme also helps your body process alcohol. Without it, alcohol doesn’t break down properly and instead turns into a toxic substance called acetaldehyde. That causes intense nausea, vomiting, and flushing. So, if you were taking this drug, you couldn’t drink at all.

More recent attempts tried to slow down sperm production, by using drugs that stop cell division, similar to certain cancer treatments. Since sperm cells are constantly being made, slowing that process can lower fertility. But these drugs came with side effects like weight gain, acne, lower testosterone, and they also weren’t very consistent.

Hormonal methods have probably come the closest to a success story. And often they are actually effective. However, they come with side effects like mood changes and hormone imbalances. What’s interesting is that these side effects, and many of the ones from earlier ideas, are very similar to ones seen in female birth control, yet male trials are often completely stopped because of them. The reason why they get away with this is because of how our society assesses risk. Female contraception is weighed against pregnancy, which has “more” serious health risks then birth control. Because men can not get pregnant, they would normally not have to endure pregnancy symptoms, so even moderate side effects are treated as a bigger issue.

Even now, the main options for male contraception haven’t changed much. Condoms are still the most common and reversible method. “Pulling out” is used but not considered reliable. Vasectomy is extremely effective, but it’s meant to be permanent and can’t always be reversed.

So, the reason male birth control isn’t widely available isn’t because scientists failed to find anything. It’s because every option so far has been refused because of gender inequality. The science keeps getting close. It just hasn’t found something that works, is safe, reversible, and fits into people’s lives and expectations all at the same time.
